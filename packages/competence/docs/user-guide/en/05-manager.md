# For Managers

Every appraisal cycle, you take on responsibilities beyond your own evaluation. As the manager your reports turn to, you start their evaluations, review their results, and help carry each one through to a closing interview. This chapter covers everything specific to your role as Manager, starting with a quick overview and then working through each responsibility in more detail.

## Your responsibilities in a cycle

Over the course of a cycle, a handful of responsibilities are yours specifically: starting evaluations for the people you manage, completing the manager review once each one reaches you, keeping your interview availability calendar current, conducting interviews for your reports — occasionally covering one for a colleague — and recording their outcomes, and keeping an eye on your team's overall progress. The sections below take each of these in turn, starting with how an evaluation begins.

## Starting an evaluation

From the **Org Chart**, find someone in your organization who doesn't currently have an active evaluation, and a Start Evaluation action appears for them — you won't find it on your own entry; you can't start your own evaluation (more on that next). Choosing it opens the **New Evaluation** screen, which shows the employee's profile alongside the current cycle's information. From here you can optionally bring in peer feedback: pick between three and five eligible colleagues, from a list that already leaves out the employee and everyone in their management chain, so every name left to choose from is a genuine peer rather than someone with authority over them. Confirm, and the evaluation opens immediately — its competency set is resolved right then, from the employee's role family, specialization, and stage-level, and frozen as of that moment. Whatever changes later in the competency configuration, this evaluation keeps the set it started with.

## Your own evaluation

You're also an Employee — every Manager is. Your own evaluation gets started the same way, just by someone else: your own manager, or occasionally a Supervisor. From there it works exactly like anyone else's — same self-assessment, same deadline, same results — all covered in the For Employees chapter. Being a Manager changes nothing about your side of it.

## The manager review

Once an evaluation reaches In Review — the employee's self-assessment is done, and any team feedback is either complete or wasn't requested — it lands on your Dashboard as a task, and you're notified. Open it, and you'll see the employee's self-grades, the team's cumulative grade if peer feedback was requested, and the team's anonymous written notes — you're the only one who ever sees those. Grade every competency, and add your own written feedback; none of it reaches the employee until their results are ready. **Save Draft** as often as you like; **Submit** once every competency is graded.

> **Note:** Unlike the self-assessment deadline, yours is a reminder, not a block — a late manager submission is never rejected.

That doesn't make it optional. Once your deadline passes, the review shows up as overdue on Supervisors' dashboards, and a Supervisor may step in and complete it on your behalf — though only by recording a reason, which stays on the evaluation's audit trail (the For Supervisors chapter covers that action, and the rest of their stall-recovery toolkit, from their side). Help can also come from inside your own reporting line: a manager above you can complete a stalled review directly, the same way you would, with no reason required, since they'd be acting as a manager rather than standing in for you. Either way, submitting computes the evaluation's scores automatically and moves it to Ready.

## Finalizing team feedback

Peer feedback has its own cycle-level deadline. If it passes and someone assigned still hasn't submitted, the evaluation doesn't have to sit stalled: you can finalize the round yourself, and so can a Supervisor, who can view the evaluation as a read-only facilitator for exactly this purpose. Finalizing works with whatever grades were actually submitted — anyone who never responded is simply left out, not counted against the employee. The action is recorded on the evaluation, and once it's done, the round counts as complete and the evaluation can move forward instead of waiting indefinitely on one non-responder.

## Your availability calendar

Open **Availability** from the sidebar for your weekly grid, bounded to the current cycle — you can't page back before the current week, or forward past the cycle's close date. Hover over an empty cell, and a small split control appears: its ✓ half marks the slot available, its ✕ half marks it busy. Click a cell you've already marked either way, and it's removed. Once a slot is booked, it turns read-only and shows the employee's name right in the cell — that's a Supervisor having booked an interview into your available time, and you can't undo it yourself.

> **Tip:** Keep your calendar current through the whole cycle, not just once interviews start getting scheduled — the more open slots a Supervisor can see, the sooner they can book one.

## Interviews — conducting and recording the outcome

The **Interviews** screen lists every evaluation that's reached Ready and is waiting on its closing interview. For any one of them, the conducting manager is whoever owns the booked calendar slot — usually you, for your own reports, but not always: if you're covering for someone, one of your open slots might get booked for a colleague's report instead, and you'll get a "team interview scheduled" notice when that happens. Either way, once the interview date has passed, record the outcome: written feedback, up to five goals for the employee's next period — each with an optional target date — and, if the conversation calls for one, a formal Performance Improvement Plan. From there, a Supervisor takes over to formally close the evaluation.

## People

**People** is the employee master/detail editor — your view into everyone's profile data. What you're able to change is permission-gated, so don't be surprised if a Supervisor can edit a field you can't. Change someone's role family or specialization, and you'll be warned with the number of their in-flight evaluations first — those keep the competency set they already started with, so nothing already underway gets disrupted. Every change you make here is recorded in the audit log.

## Team analytics

**Team analytics**, under Insights, gives you seven reports scoped to your own reporting line: coverage, interview timing, self vs manager alignment, a competence heatmap, score distribution by level, performance drivers, and grader calibration. Every chart carries a "how it's calculated" note if you want the detail behind a number. As on every analytics screen in the application, a group smaller than three people is never shown at all — hidden outright, so nobody can be identified by working backward from a small statistic.
