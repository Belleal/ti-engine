# For Team Members

Team Member isn't a role you hold all the time — it's conferred on you for one colleague's evaluation, whenever the person starting it decides peer feedback would help. This chapter covers what that involves: being picked, giving your feedback, how anonymous it stays, and why your honest input matters to the result.

## You've been asked for peer feedback

When your colleague's evaluation starts, whoever begins it — usually their manager, occasionally a Supervisor — can choose to bring in peer feedback. If they do, they pick between three and five colleagues to provide it, and you're one of them this time.

Eligibility isn't arbitrary: it excludes the person being evaluated, of course, but also their whole management chain — their manager, and every manager above that manager in the reporting line. That keeps the team round made up of genuine peers, never anyone who holds authority over the person being reviewed.

While your feedback is pending, it shows up as a task on your Dashboard, so you always know it's waiting on you.

## Giving your feedback

Open the evaluation either from that dashboard task or by finding your colleague in the **Org Chart**. Once you're in, a role banner at the top confirms you're grading as Team — the same screen your colleague and their manager use, just showing you a different view.

By default, team grading is collective: instead of grading each competency one at a time, you give a single grade for each of the nine subcategories, and it's automatically applied to every competency inside that subcategory. (Your organization can configure this differently, so that team members grade competency by competency instead.)

While you're grading, you see no one else's grades at all — not the employee's self-assessment, not any other team member's input. You're forming your own independent view. Alongside your grades, you may add a short written note if you'd like to add context a grade alone can't carry.

## Anonymity — what happens to your input

Your individual grades are never shown to anyone — not the employee, not even the manager. Once every assigned team member has submitted, all the individual grades for each competency are averaged into a single cumulative grade, rounded to the nearest grade value, and it's that combined figure — never your personal grade — that ever appears anywhere else.

Your written note follows a similar principle but isn't merged with anyone else's: it goes to the manager as anonymous feedback, with nothing identifying you attached. The employee never sees it, individually or otherwise.

> **Note:** This anonymity isn't temporary — it holds for the life of the evaluation. Even after your colleague's results are finalized and the evaluation is Closed, your individual grades are still never shown to the employee or the manager; only the combined cumulative grade ever is.

## Deadline and finalizing

You can submit your feedback once, at any point before the team-feedback deadline set on the cycle. As soon as every assigned team member has submitted, the round completes on its own and the evaluation moves forward.

If the deadline passes and someone still hasn't submitted, that's not necessarily a dead end: the evaluation's manager, or a Supervisor, can finalize the round anyway so it isn't held up by one non-responder. Anything already submitted still counts toward the cumulative grade. In the rare case where nobody on the team submitted at all, the round is simply left out of scoring entirely — excluded, not treated as a zero, so it never drags your colleague's score down.

## Why it matters

When a team round happens, it carries 30% of the final score — a real, meaningful share alongside the employee's own self-assessment and their manager's review. Because your grades are anonymous and never traced back to you, there's no reason to soften them, and no reason to inflate them either — the most useful thing you can do is grade honestly against what the scope description says is expected at your colleague's level, the same standard everyone else is held to.
